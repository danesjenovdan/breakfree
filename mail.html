Dear Member of European Parliament,

a major reform of copyright law is currently under way in the EU, which aims to modernize and harmonize certain important aspects of EU copyright legislation and adapt it to the challenges of the digital age. In September 2016 the Commission released a package of legislation proposals, most important of which is the Proposal for a Directive of the European Parliament and of the Council on Copyright in the Digital Single Market.

At the end of May, following intense negotiations, held far from the eyes of EU citizens, the Member States have granted a mandate to the Presidency of the EU Council to submit a compromise text of the Directive to the European Parliament, including very problematic provisions in Article 11 and 13. Consequently, the only possibility to protect the interests of EU citizens against a handful of individuals in the publishing and entertainment industries, lies in the hands of the European Parliament, more concretely in Your hands.

To our great concern the lobbies of rights-holders, especially in the publishing and entertainment industries, have managed to persuade the majority of Member States to consent to unbalanced legislation, which sets the interest of the rights-holders above the needs of EU citizens, internet users, research and educational institutions and cultural heritage institutions. The Proposal for a Directive, on which a vote will be held on the Parliament on the 21. of June, has failed to address the main shortcomings of the original Commission proposal, and in some cases the compromise proposal, adopted by the Member States, makes matters even worse.

One of the most problematic provisions is Article 13, which introduces new obligations for providers of internet platforms, which allow sharing of user uploaded content (like videos, music, texts, etc.). The platforms would be able to avoid the repercussions of copyright infringement only by entering into licences with rights-holders, or alternatively by filtering and blocking all content which could potentially be copyright infringing. Such general content filtering obligations, also referred to as "censorship machines", can limit the freedom of speech, a fundamental human right, and open doors to private censorship of user content. In the past, EU citizens have already refused similar attempts, like in the case of ACTA, but these seem to return now in more complex fashion.

Under pressure from the German publishing lobby the Member States have consented to the introduction of a new ancillary copyright for press publishers (Article 11), which grants them the right to remuneration for any use, even private use, of content or parts of content (like for the posting of a link to a certain news article), even though more than 160 academics – specialists have advised against this novelty and called on Member States to oppose it. The payment of remuneration would burden journalists, photographers and many non-institutional media publishers, to the advantage of big publishing houses and press agencies. New and unnecessary rights for press publishers will shift the balance in the media market towards big players, cripple the freedom of expression on the internet and independent journalism and promote the use of unverified information, thus aiding in the rise of "fake news".

Member States were in favour of a narrow and innovation-hostile exception for Text and Data Mining (Article 3), which could limit the competitiveness of EU companies in the area of machine learning and artificial intelligence. The Proposal for a Directive introduces a mandatory exception for TDM, but mainly for the benefit of research institutions and for research purposes only. Other users (media, NGO's, companies, etc.) can only make use of the exception if the member states adopt an optional exception on their behalf as well.

For educational institutions the biggest disappointment is the proposal for an optional regime allowing the use of copyrighted works for teaching (Article 4). The Proposal for a Directive contains an exception, which allows for free use of works for illustration for teaching, but the member states can decide not to implement this exception and opt for a licensing regime instead, which will require schools to pay remuneration to rights-holders, if licences are easily available on the market. Such payable licences will pose a substantial financial burden for public and private educational institutions and force them to negotiate with collective management organizations and even individual rights holders, when they are not represented collectively.

To our regret, during negotiations in the EU Council the majority of Member States did not care for user rights, but see copyright as an instrument for the protection of existing business models in the publishing and entertainment industries. Because the debate on the level of member states is finished, the fate of internet users, research and educational institutions, start-ups and media is now in the hands of the European Parliament and the elected MEP's. You have to stand up for harmonised exceptions, which will promote research and innovation, enable quality education in digital environments and provide access to cultural heritage.

After the concluded negotiations in the Commission and Council the European Parliament is the last threshold to protect the interests of EU citizens against a handful of individuals in the publishing and entertainment industries.

We call upon You, a member of the European Parliament, to demand the deletion of Article 11 and the new and harmful new right contained in it and the deletion of Article 13, which imposes new burdens on platforms and users and allows for content censorship, as well as to stand for mandatory and broader exceptions for TDM and education as currently proposed in the Proposal of the Council.

Spoštovani poslanec/poslanka Evropskega Parlamenta,

v Evropski uniji trenutno poteka velika reforma avtorskega prava, katere namen je posodobiti in poenotiti nekatere pomembne vidike avtorskega prava v EU ter jih prilagoditi izzivom digitalne dobe. Septembra 2016 je Evropska komisija objavila paket zakonodajnih predlogov, katerega najpomembnejši del je Predlog direktive Evropskega parlamenta in Sveta o avtorskih pravicah na enotnem digitalnem trgu.

Po intenzivnih pogajanjih, ki so potekala daleč od oči državljanov EU, so države članice konec maja Predsedstvu Sveta EU podelile mandat za predložitev kompromisnega besedila Direktive v odločanje Evropskemu Parlamentu, kar vključuje tudi mandat za nekatere zelo problematične člene, kot sta npr. člena 11 in 13. Zato je edina možnost zaščite interesov državljanov EU pred peščico posameznikov v založniški in zabavni industriji v rokah Evropskega parlamenta, bolj konkretno v Vaših rokah.

Z zaskrbljenostjo ugotavljamo, da je uspelo lobijem imetnikov pravic, zlasti založnikom in producentom audio in video vsebin, prepričati večino držav članic, da so privolile v neuravnoteženo zakonodajo, ki interese imetnikov pravic postavlja nad potrebe državljanov EU, uporabnikov interneta, raziskovalnih in izobraževalnih institucij in ustanov za varstvo kulturne dediščine. Predlog Direktive, o katerem se bo 21. junija odločalo v Evropskem Parlamentu, ni ustrezno odpravil glavnih pomanjkljivosti originalnega predloga Evropske Komisije, v določenih primerih pa je končni predlog, ki so ga izpogajale države članice, celo še slabši.

Ena izmed najbolj problematičnih je določba člena 13, ki naj bi uvedla nove obveznosti za ponudnike spletnih platform, na katere lahko uporabniki nalagajo različne vsebine (npr. glasbo, video posnetke, besedila, itd.). Platforme se bi sankcijam za kršitve avtorskih pravic lahko izognile le tako, da bi z imetniki pravic sklenile ustrezne licenčne pogodbe ali pa tako, da bi filtrirale in blokirale nalaganje vsebin, ki bi lahko pomenile kršitev avtorskih pravic. Tako generalno filtriranje vsebin, velikokrat govorimo kar o ”cenzorskem stroju”, lahko krši svobodo govora, ki je ena izmed temeljnih človekovih pravic, in odpira vrata za privatno cenzuro nad uporabniškimi vsebinami. V preteklosti so se državljani EU podobnim iniciativam, kakršen je bil npr. sporazum ACTA, že množično uprli, vendar se zdaj očitno vračajo v veliko bolj razdelani obliki.

Pod pritiski nemškega založniškega lobija so države članice podprle uvedbo nove sorodne pravice za izdajatelje medijskih publikacij (člen 11), s katero bi ti pridobili pravico do nadomestila za vsakršno, tudi zasebno rabo vsebin ali celo zgolj delov teh vsebin (npr. za objavo linka do določene novice), kljub temu, da je več kot 160 akademikov – strokovnjakov, opozarjalo na nevarnosti tega predloga in pozivalo, da države članice uvedbe te nove pravice ne podprejo. Plačilo nadomestil bi obremenilo novinarje, fotografe in mnoge neinstitucionalne izdajatelje novic, koristilo pa bi zlasti večjim medijskim hišam in tiskovnim agencijam. Nove nepotrebne pravice za izdajatelje novic bi premaknile razmerje moči v prid velikih akterjev na medijskem trgu, krnile svobodo izražanja na internetu in neodvisno novinarstvo ter spodbujale uporabo nepreverjenih virov informacij in s tem porast "fake news".

Države članice so podprle ozko in inovacijam neprilagojeno izjemo za tekstovno in podatkovno rudarjenje (člen 3), ki bo, če bo sprejeta v tej obliki, krnila konkurenčnost EU podjetij, ki se ukvarjajo s strojnim učenjem in umetno inteligenco. Predlog Direktive namreč prosto tekstovno in podatkovno rudarjenje po zakonito dostopnih podatkih omogoča zlasti raziskovalnim institucijam v znanstvene namene, ostalim uporabnikom (npr. medijem, nevladnim organizacijam, zasebnim podjetjem) pa le, če se države članice odločijo uvesti opcijsko izjemo tudi zanje.

Z vidika izobraževalnih institucij je največje razočaranje uvedba izbirnega režima za uporabo avtorskih del pri pouku (člen 4). Predlog Direktive predvideva izjemo, ki omogoča prosto in brezplačno uporabo avtorskih del za namene poučevanja, vendar pa se države članice lahko odločijo, da takšne izjeme ne implementirajo in raje uvedejo režim licenciranja, v skladu s katerim bodo šole za uporabo del pri pouku morale imetnikom pravic plačati nadomestilo, kadar bodo na trgu licence enostavno dostopne. Takšne plačljive licence bodo znatno finančno obremenile javne in zasebne izobraževalne institucije ter prisilile te institucije v pogajanja s kolektivnimi organizacijami ali celo posameznimi imetniki pravic, kadar ti niso kolektivno zastopani.

Z zaskrbljenostjo ugotavljamo, da večini ostalih držav članic v postopku usklajevanja predloga Direktive ni bilo mar za pravice uporabnikov, temveč vidijo avtorske pravice kot instrument za zaščito poslovnih modelov v založniški, glasbeni in filmski industriji. Ker je zakonodajna razprava na ravni držav članic zaključena, je usoda uporabnikov interneta, raziskovalnih in izobraževalnih ustanov, nevladnih organizacij, start-upov in medijev v rokah Evropskega parlamenta in izvoljenih evropskih poslancev. Ti se morajo zavzeti za poenotene izjeme od avtorskih pravic, ki bodo spodbujale raziskave in inovacije, omogočale kvalitetno izobraževanje v digitalnih okoljih in dostop do dobrin kulturne dediščine.

Po zaključenih pogajanjih na ravni Evropske Komisije in Sveta EU so poslanci Evropskega Parlamenta zadnji branik interesov državljanov EU proti peščici posameznikov v založniški in zabavni industriji.

Pozivamo Vas, da se zavzamete za izbris člena 11, ki prinaša škodljivo novo sorodno pravico, in za izbris člena 13, ki uvaja nova bremena za platforme in uporabnike ter lahko omogoča cenzuriranje vsebin, ter se zavzamete za obvezne in široke izjeme za izjeme za tekstovno in podatkovno rudarjenje in za izobraževanje.
